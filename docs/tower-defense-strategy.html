What Is a Tower Defense Game?

Enemies follow a path toward a point you're defending (your base, a village, a portal). You build towers along the path that automatically attack enemies as they pass. Enemies drop gold when killed. You spend gold to build more towers or upgrade existing ones. Waves get harder. You lose if too many enemies reach the end.

The tension comes from resource scarcity: you never have enough gold to build everything you want. Every purchase is a trade-off. Do you build a new tower or upgrade an existing one? Do you invest in economy (gold collectors) or defense (more DPS)?

Key difference from Bullet Survivors: In BS, you ARE the weapon — you move, dodge, and your upgrades modify YOUR stats. In TD, you're the architect — you design a defense system and watch it execute. The satisfaction shifts from reflexes to strategy.

Game Design Concepts

1. Phase Structure

Unlike Bullet Survivors (continuous action), TD games have two distinct phases:

Build Phase

Time stops (or slows). You survey the battlefield, place/upgrade towers, plan your defense. This is where strategy happens.

Duration: unlimited or timed. Your call as creative director.

Wave Phase

Enemies spawn and march. Towers fire automatically. You watch your strategy play out, maybe make emergency purchases. This is where strategy is tested.

Can you build during waves? Another creative decision.

Creative decision for you: Some TDs let you build during waves (Bloons TD). Some force you to commit before the wave starts (Kingdom Rush). Building during waves is more forgiving but less strategic. Which do you prefer?

2. Tower Types

Towers are the core expression of player strategy. Each type has a role — the key is that no single tower works for everything, forcing diverse placement.

Archetype Role Strength Weakness Example
Shooter Single-target DPS High damage per shot One enemy at a time Arrow tower
Splash Area damage Hits groups Low single-target Cannon, bomb tower
Slow Crowd control Makes everything else better No damage alone Ice tower, mud trap
Sniper Long range priority Huge range + damage Very slow fire rate Lightning spire
Economy Gold generation Pays for itself over time Does nothing defensively Gold mine, tax collector
Creative decisions for you: What are YOUR tower types? The archetypes above are the genre standard, but you could invent new ones. A tower that teleports enemies backward? A tower that buffs adjacent towers? A tower that converts enemies? These are your choices.

3. Enemy Types

Enemies exist to invalidate one-dimensional strategies. Each type should force the player to diversify.

Archetype What it does Countered by
Standard Walks at normal speed, normal HP Anything (baseline)
Fast Runs past slow towers Slow towers + splash
Tank Absorbs massive damage High DPS (sniper, upgraded shooter)
Swarm Many small enemies at once Splash damage
Flying Ignores walls and ground towers Anti-air towers (forces build diversity)
Shield Immune to first N hits / has armor Sustained DPS, armor-piercing
Healer Heals nearby enemies Sniper (priority targeting)

The interplay between tower types and enemy types IS the strategy. A well-designed TD has moments where the player says "I need to build X because the next wave has Y."

4. Economy Design

The economy is the pacing mechanism. Too much gold = no tension (build everything). Too little = no agency (can't adapt).

Income sources

Kill gold: Each enemy drops gold on death. Base income. Scales with enemy difficulty.
Wave bonus: Bonus gold for completing a wave with no leaks. Rewards clean play.
Interest/passive: Gold per second or per wave from economy towers. Rewards early investment.
Player-controlled: "Send next wave early for bonus gold" — risk/reward trade-off.

Spending categories

New towers: Expand coverage. High upfront cost.
Tower upgrades: Improve existing towers. Usually more efficient than new towers.
Walls: Redirect enemy pathing. Cheap but strategic.
Special abilities: One-time powers (airstrike, freeze all, heal base). Burst spending.

Creative decisions for you: How expensive are towers? Can you sell towers for a refund (full? partial? none?)? Is there interest on banked gold? Can you "speed up" waves for bonus gold? These dials control the game's feel: generous economy = casual fun, tight economy = hardcore strategy.

5. Map & Path Design

Three fundamental approaches:

Fixed Path

Enemies follow a predefined route. Towers placed alongside. Simpler to design, easier for players to read.

Examples: Kingdom Rush, Plants vs Zombies

Maze Building

Open field. Enemies pathfind to the exit. Players build walls to create mazes, forcing longer paths. Deep strategy.

Examples: Desktop TD, Gemcraft

Hybrid (Recommended)

Fixed entry/exit points with open areas where players can build walls to redirect the path. Enemies pathfind dynamically. Best of both: readable structure + strategic wall placement.

Examples: Bloons TD, Element TD

Creative decision for you: Fixed path is simpler to implement (no pathfinding needed). Maze building is more strategic but requires A* pathfinding. The hybrid is the most satisfying but the most complex. Your call — this shapes the entire game feel.

6. Progression & Replayability

What makes a player start a new run?

  • Tower upgrades: Each tower has 2-3 upgrade tiers. Upgrading transforms the tower (new visual, new ability). Mid-run progression.
  • Difficulty scaling: Waves get harder. Enemy stats scale. New enemy types introduced. Tension escalates.
  • Score/rating: Stars per level (no leaks = 3 stars). Completionists replay for perfect scores.
  • Unlock system: Beating levels unlocks new tower types or abilities for future runs. Cross-run progression.
  • Endless mode: After the campaign waves, keep going until you lose. Leaderboard-style.

New Game Dev Concepts (Not in Bullet Survivors)

What you'll learn building this

1. Grid / Tile System New

Towers snap to a grid. The game world is divided into cells (e.g., 32x32 pixels). Each cell is either empty, path, tower, or wall. Mouse position converts to grid coordinates: gridX = Math.floor(mouseX / CELL_SIZE).

Why it matters: Every builder game (Factorio, SimCity, Minecraft) uses a grid. This is the foundation of any "place stuff in a world" mechanic.

2. Pathfinding (A*) New

If you allow maze building / wall placement, enemies need to find the shortest path around obstacles. A* is the standard algorithm: it explores nodes in order of "distance traveled + estimated distance remaining." It finds the optimal path efficiently.

Why it matters: Pathfinding is one of the most universally useful algorithms in game dev. Every RTS, every RPG with AI movement, every navigation system uses some form of A*. Learning it here means you can use it everywhere.

Note: If you choose fixed paths (no maze building), you can skip A* entirely. The path is just an array of waypoints that enemies follow in order.

3. Game State Machine New

Bullet Survivors had three states: running, paused (level-up), game over. Tower defense has more: main menu → build phase → wave phase → wave complete → build phase → ... → game over. A state machine formalizes this: each state defines what update/render logic runs, what input is valid, and what transitions are allowed.

Why it matters: Every game with menus, cutscenes, inventories, or dialogue uses a state machine. It's the standard way to manage "what mode is the game in right now?"

4. Mouse/Click Interaction New

Bullet Survivors was keyboard-only. TD requires mouse: click to select tower type, click to place, click to select placed tower, click to upgrade. This introduces hit-testing ("what did the player click on?"), hover states, drag previews, and coordinate conversion (screen → world → grid).

Why it matters: Most games beyond pure action use mouse/touch interaction. The coordinate conversion chain (screen → camera → world → grid) is the key skill.

5. Tower Targeting AI New

Each tower decides which enemy to shoot. Common strategies:

  • First: Target the enemy closest to the exit (most dangerous)
  • Closest: Target the nearest enemy to the tower (highest chance to hit)
  • Strongest: Target the highest-HP enemy (focus fire)
  • Weakest: Target the lowest-HP enemy (secure kills for gold)

Why it matters: AI decision-making is a core game dev skill. Targeting priority is a simple version of the broader problem "how do game entities make decisions?"

6. Range Visualization New

When placing a tower, show its attack range as a translucent circle. When hovering over a placed tower, show the same. This is information design — making the game's rules visible so the player can make informed decisions.

Why it matters: Games communicate constantly through visual feedback. Range circles, valid/invalid placement colors, path highlighting — these are all forms of "show, don't tell." This is the UI/UX skill that makes games intuitive vs confusing.

7. Economy System New

Track gold, validate purchases ("can the player afford this?"), handle refunds on tower selling. Sounds simple but it's a resource management system — the same pattern used in every game with currencies, inventories, or crafting.

Your Role: Creative Director

Decisions that need your vision

In Bullet Survivors, the genre dictated most decisions — Vampire Survivors is well-understood, and the implementation order was clear. Tower defense is a broader genre with more room for creative interpretation. Here's what you'll need to decide:

Theme & Setting

Medieval castle? Sci-fi base? Fantasy dungeon? The theme determines art direction, tower names, enemy designs, and narrative framing.

Map Philosophy

Fixed paths, open mazes, or hybrid? Single map or campaign of maps? This is the biggest architectural decision — it determines whether you need pathfinding.

Tower Design

What are your towers? What do they do? What are their upgrade paths? This is the creative core — the towers ARE the game.

Economy Feel

Generous (build lots, experiment freely) or tight (every purchase matters, mistakes are costly)? This controls casual vs hardcore feel.

Player Role

Pure architect (place towers only)? Or also a hero unit you control directly (like in Bullet Survivors)? Your original idea included a player character who can run out and fight — this blends genres in an interesting way.

Your "Hook"

What makes YOUR TD different? You mentioned: buildable walls, buyable units (gold collectors), a controllable player character. That's a genre blend — part TD, part RTS, part action. That could be your unique angle.

Next Steps

  1. Pick a theme and setting. This doesn't affect architecture but it affects everything about how the game feels.
  2. Decide: fixed path or maze building? This is the biggest technical fork. Fixed path = simpler, faster to build. Maze = deeper strategy, needs A*.
  3. Sketch your tower ideas. Even just names and one-line descriptions. "Ice Tower: slows enemies in range. Cannon: splash damage, slow fire rate."
  4. Decide on the player-character angle. Pure TD? Or the hybrid you described (player can run out, fight, collect gold)?
